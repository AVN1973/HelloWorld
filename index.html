ProgWorld:Recommendations

The Purpose of ProgWorld

I, the creator of this page, designed it with the primary intention of helping those aspiring fans and connoisseurs of Progressive music along the path to familiarity with the art form. Not only with the albums and bands of prog, but with the stories and ideas each song aims to convey. I intend to do this by presenting a set of bands and albums I consider to be not only essential, but revolutionary within the genre; the cream of the crop within a medium already oversaturated with innovation and originality.

Reviews and Reasons

Selling England by the pound

Elegant, Avant-Garde, and just a bit gloomy, the cover of this album sums up the music very well. Almost giving you the impression that it will be predictable, until you place it in your record player and drop the needle. Fifty-three minutes later you will undoubtedly find yourself longing to return to the magical world you just weightlessly floated through. A bizarre, noticeably British wonderland full to the brim with magical keyboard solos, ethereal guitar harmonies, and some of the most haunting poetry ever to exit a human's heart. Eight vastly unique, individual auditory metropolises dot the grassy English landscape, each evoking different emotions from those who travel through them on their journey. I wish I could attempt to describe each of these solitary utopias to one who had not experienced them with their own senses, but I would be more than unable to do them any sort of justice. With that in mind, I can tell you their names. There are the four lesser wonders: I know what I like (in your wardrobe), More Fool Me, After the Ordeal, and Aisle of plenty. Then there are the four greater wonders: Dancing with the Moonlit knight, Firth of Fifth, the Battle of Epping Forest, and the Cinema Show. It is important to keep in mind the fact that while I am no trained and seasoned music critic, I have listened to dozens and dozens of Progressive Rock albums, yet none have come close to the power and completeness of this 1973 Genesis masterpiece.

Images and Words

In the nineties, a musical era widely known for minimalistic, pragmatic rock and metal with a heavy DIY mindset, Progressive Rock was definitely not in style. Bands like Nirvana, Green Day, and Rage Against the Machine emphasized message over musicianship, and viewed technical/theoretical proficiency as a waste of time (especially when you could get the same message across with three chords in 4/4). But as always, there were those who would never forget the masterpieces of the seventies, and attempted to carry on the proud torch of Prog, experimenting and innovating with contemporary musical norms. In my opinion, Dream Theater is the best example of the Prog mindset in the modern day, and the 1992 masterpiece Images and words their crowning achievement. This album is the love of music lovers, and the bane of novice musicians everywhere, and you can expect all eight songs to bring you to tears as much as they will destroy your confidence in your own musical abilities. Combining 80's mainstream metal, prog in the vein of Yes, and each member's musical genius, Images and Words is easily my top album ever from 1980 to now. Even compared to the Rest of Dream Theater's highly impressive discography, this release stands out as their most emotionally real, and the most well prioritized. As Dream Theater have stated in various interviews that the primary elements they strive to focus on in their music are:

  • Metal
  • Melody
  • Progressive Roots

This record laid the groundwork for modern Prog, despite the fact that nothing has topped it since.

The Dark side of the moon

Run, rabbit run, Dig that hole, forget the sun, and when at last the work is done, don't sit down it's time to dig another one...

It's very easy to get lost in the bleak, hyperrealistic lyrics of Pink Floyd, easily labeling them as perfect already and unable to be topped. That is, until you take a mental step back and percieve the dreamy cloud of mellotron and guitar upon which the lyrics reside, and it's about at that point that you realize that this is your new favorite album. Pink Floyd's 1973 somber masterpiece is considered one of the best albums ever, and while the individual songs are excellent by themselves, it is when the album is listened to and judged as a unit that it really becomes timeless. From the haunting heartbeats of Speak to Me, to the profound proclaim in "Eclipse" that there is actually no Dark side of the moon, the whole album flows as smoothly as a river of rainbows. Easily the least technical album on this list, yet some days the most meaningful, Pink Floyd's distinctly minimalistic Prog will continue to inspire far into distant the future.

Damnation

Opeth's first album to not feature growls is more likely to grab you by your heart than your headbanging neck, and once it has pulled you in, you're likely to be under its complete control until the last ghostly hums on "Weakness". At which point the spell will be broken, in favor of a lingering sense of beautiful melancholia, knowing Opeth's eclectic, beautiful nightmare will only be as far away as your headphones. Drawing influence from Pink Floyd, Steven Wilson, and Jazz, the instrumentation on this album is definitely the highlight, as distortion-free guitars and easily audible basses create melodies you won't want to stop hearing. However, this is not to discredit or even overlook Mikael Akerfeldt's amazing clean singing, as his lyrics and beautiful baritone voice mix so excellently with the music that it makes you wonder if he himself is an instrument.

Close to the edge

A spiritual manifesto composed by virtuosos, a three-piece neoclassical symphony, and the record containing the best song of all time. There are mixed opinions on Yes's fifth studio album, yet all who have listened to it consider it a masterpiece, and almost all consider it the essential album for budding Prog fans. (which is why I chose to add in the album cover as a link to the highlight song: Close to the Edge) Aside from being voted the greatest album ever by Prog magazine, this album is notable for the amazing musicians on it; easily the predecessors of Dream Theater in their skill, feel, and innovation. The year 1972 was an especially good one for Yes, as their iconic lineup was in place, and the legends Steve Howe, Chris Squire, and Rick Wakeman played guitar, bass, and keyboards respectively. The level of teamwork and cohesion the band experienced during this era of their career would never attained again, despite many other excellent albums being released in Yes's future.

As this is the end of the informational section of this site, I leave you aspiring Fans of the genre with one piece of advice: click the image of Yes's Close to the Edge album and begin your journey into this beloved art form!
